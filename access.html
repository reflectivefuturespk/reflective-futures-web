Access

Underpinning any history of transportation lies the notion of mobility--and therefore of access. Where communities can afford to rent or own housing, where they are able to attend school, and where they may work to earn a living; each of these parameters are shaped by and give shape to the ways that people move about the city.

Access: 1870's - 1970's

Throughout the 19th century, the City of Poughkeepsie and the entire state of New York conformed to de-facto systems of racial segregation. Yet in May of 1873, New York became one of the first states to complement the Fourteenth Amendment with its own civil rights statute, officially disallowing discrimination in public schools. In the fall of 1873, Joseph Rhodes, the owner of Eagle Dyeing Establishment, enrolled his two daughters in an all-white elementary school, and despite considerable protest from white families, Josephine Rhodes became the first black graduate of Poughkeepsie High School by 1879.

Image Source: Valentine & Sons Publishing Co. 1912

This early narrative of progressivism belies a stubborn persistence of racist housing and commercial practices that would continue to punctuate Poughkeepsie’s history to varied effect for years to come. Before World War II, major hotels and boarding houses in Poughkeepsie were known to refuse accommodations to black guests, including well known artists Marian Anderson and Langston Hughes at the city’s beloved Nelson House Hotel. The 1920s saw a resurgence of racial terrorism with an active chapter of the Ku Klux Klan making a number of public demonstrations in Dutchess County.

Image Source: Times Herald. 1924

Throughout the bulk of the 20th century, the social barriers to people of color took one spatial form through discriminatory mortgage lending practices. Now commonly referenced as “redlining,” banks and real estate professionals created Residential Security Maps to assess lending risk throughout urban environments through which neighborhoods were judged to be A through D -- ”Best,” “Still Desirable,” “Definitely Declining,” or “Hazardous.” In addition, homeowners often included Restrictive Covenants in their deeds of sale to prevent purchase by non-white families.

Image Source: Mapping Inequality. 1938

During the Great Depression of the 1930s, the Federal Home Loan Bank Board (FHLBB), organized a now-infamous program of redlining based on the social and economic demographics of current residents. The City of Poughkeepsie was one of 239 cities to participate in this program nationwide.

Image Source: Mapping Inequality. 1938

Residential Security Maps were the product of the prevailing theories of urban growth at the time, such as the Burgess Urban Land Use Model, or ecological model, pictured here. The central philosophy is that as housing declines over time, it also filters down a social hierarchy organized by race and class. Through this model, poverty and non-white communities were understood to be pathologies that could be predicted and dealt with according to the logic of disease. The rhetoric of this model would go on to inform the slum clearance policies championed during the era of urban renewal.

Image Source: Rutan. 2016

In the face of these structural barriers, key community organizations in Poughkeepsie have a long history of creating opportunities where they have not otherwise existed. This is a photo of an after-school group at the Catharine Street Community Center from 1922. Since the founding efforts of Mrs. Samuel Buck in the early 1900s, this organization has supplemented educational opportunities for young, underserved children of color with the aim to enable their meaningful involvement in civic and community activities.

Image Source: Catherine Street Community Center. 1922.

The Civil Rights Movement of the 1960s brought issues of racial justice into new visibility.Viewing Poughkeepsie’s progress in the context of our nation’s broader struggles, students from Vassar College picketed the Woolworth’s store on Main Street on March 17, 1960 to stand in solidarity with protests against lunch counter segregation in the chain’s southern stores. The Fair Housing Act of 1968 would finally prohibit discrimination concerning the sale, rental and financing of housing based on race, religion, national origin or sex.

Image Source: Vassar Archives. 1960
