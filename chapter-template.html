Battle Over the East-West Arterial   1947-1979

The first plans for an east-west arterial were developed in 1947 when the New York State Department of Public Works proposed to create a recessed highway, intersecting the proposed north-south arterial with a large cloverleaf at the approach to the Mid-Hudson Bridge. The city’s budgeting concerns stalled the construction of both highways, however, and the east-west component was put on hiatus after traffic was partially alleviated by the expansion of the Taconic State Parkway in in 1952.

Image Source: NY State Department of Public Works, 1947

In 1960 City of Poughkeepsie approved a General Development Plan which redesigned the arterials into a loop system. The plan was intended to inform city planning decisions over the next two decades and placed high priority upon new construction to account for an expanding suburban population. With one-way arterials proposed to overlay Mill and Church Streets, the loop system was meant to alleviate traffic on congested streets while anticipating increased traffic flows from numerous directions.

Image Source: Candeub & Fleissig, 1960

The 1960 General Development Plan coordinated this new loop system with urban renewal plans for redevelopment (in blue) and rehabilitation (in yellow). Taking advantage of the federal-aid programs made possible through the Housing Act of 1949 and 1954, large swaths of the city could be condemned as “blight” before being appropriated through eminent domain for demolition. By marrying plans for improved traffic circulation to campaigns intended to clean up the urban core, planners believed they would attract private commercial development and thereby ensure the City of Poughkeepsie’s steady growth.

Image Source: Candeub & Fleissig, 1960

In 1964, Governor Rockefeller passed legislation that relieved cities in New York State from having to pay the cost to attain rights-of-way for highway projects. With these budgeting concerns offset to the state, Poughkeepsie could again move forward with plans for an east-west arterial while the north-south arterial at Route 9 was already underway. By 1966, planners at the State Department of Transportation worked with the city to specify plans for the loop system, which proposed to widen Mill and Church Streets and slice new paths through some residential neighborhoods to meet Routes 44/55 in the Town of Poughkeepsie. The plan called for the creation of 30 cul-de-sacs, blocking both vehicular and pedestrian access to the Central Business District along Main Street.

Image Source: City of Poughkeepsie, 1966
