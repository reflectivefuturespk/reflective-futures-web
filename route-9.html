Route 9

Urban renewal describes an era of federal aid programs that radically reshaped urban landscapes across the United States between 1949 and 1974. The term was coined by the Housing Act of 1954, yet the push to build interstate highways was underway at the same time. The Housing Act and the Highway Act enabled local governments to clear significant portions urban land - fast. In Poughkeepsie, hundreds of older buildings and streetscapes were lost under the banner of modernization, displacing the communities that called them home.

Route 9 Overtakes Union Square: 1947-1966

In 1944, New York State passed an arterial highway law, which allowed the Department of Public Works to develop highway plans through cities. The law called for the state to pay for the entire cost of construction, and to split right-of-way acquisition costs with the city. These maps, generated through a 1947 report for Poughkeepsie, roughly outline the present day Route 9 as it was originally planned. By diverting truck traffic away from the commercial core, the state believed that Route 9 would prepare the city for a projected population of 80,000, anticipating future land use and business needs of the rapidly growing city.

Image Source: New York State Department of Public Works. 1947

The biggest determining factor for Route 9’s location was the Mid-Hudson Bridge, which emptied into Union Square, a bustling hub of commerce and traffic in the heart of a residential area. Shortly after the construction of the bridge, traffic problems ensued, and the need for a smooth transition between east-west and north-south automobile traffic became evident.  

Image Source: Hudson Valley Historic Bridges, date unknown

The arterial was redesigned 1954-1956, as the 1947 plan quickly became unpopular with city leaders and residents. The state hired New York City engineering firm King and Gavaris to provide an updated survey and plans for the arterial that had less of a destructive impact on the community of Wards 1 and 2. John DeLaney, a champion of downtown citizens’ interests, became a member of the Poughkeepsie Planning commission in 1952 and voiced community concerns over the highway’s planned destruction of prominent community spaces, including North Clover Street, the Church of the Holy Comforter and Mount Carmel Square. The redesign of the arterial more closely followed the railroad, and minimized the cloverleaf interchange, skirting Mount Carmel and the Church of the Holy Comforter, while still destroying 300 dwelling units.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Journal. 1956

Although originally planned in 1947, the expansion of Route 9 through the City of Poughkeepsie would not begin for another 16 years due to the city’s inability to cover half the cost to acquire rights-of-way. Citing the location of the Mid-Hudson Bridge as a major contributing factor to the city’s need for an expanded interchange, the city pursued support from the New York State Bridges Association. After a long legislative battle throughout the 1950s, the Bridge Association pledged $1.5 million towards the project, leaving the city responsible for only $575,000.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Journal. 1958

The state’s release of the plans in 1956 confirmed where destruction would potentially occur. Although a public hearing was held to solicit the public’s opinion, some noted that the timing of the afternoon hearing would inhibit the community’s ability to oppose plans, as the largely working class residents would have to give up an afternoon’s wages to attend the meeting. As legislative battles for funding delayed construction, community attitudes changed by 1959. Due to the speculation of highway construction, construction dates remained uncertain, which affected building maintenance and rental income. In 1959, 278 people living in Route 9’s path petitioned Common Council to make a plan for highway construction as soon as possible. By 1960 acquisitions were underway, but residents reported not receiving fair compensation for their properties.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Journal. 1958

In January of 1961, the Poughkeepsie Journal ran a series of features on the history of the places that were slated to be destroyed by Route 9. Union Square, the intersection of four streets (Union, Church, Clover, and Gates), was one of these places. Its significance lies with the early history of Poughkeepsie, where one of the oldest road connections from downtown to the waterfront at Union Street remained. Over time, this hub has stood witness to numerous changes in Poughkeepsie’s urban environment, most importantly with the Mid-Hudson Bridge. The loss of this hub may have temporarily solved traffic tensions, but the loss of community institutions and physical connections would be difficult to recover.

Image Source: (1) Poughkeepsie Journal 1961; (2) Historic Bridges of the Hudson Valley, date unknown

Union Square’s destruction was swift. Businesses were often left with inadequate compensation and close eviction dates. Lindmark’s Bookstore, owned by 72-year-old John Lindmark on lower Church Street near the Mid-Hudson Bridge, unfortunately faced this swift destruction to dramatic effect. Lindmark was unable to foot the bill for moving his book collection valued at $1.25 million in 18 months. Because Lindmark only received $22,000 for his property, his demolition crews were forced to leave his collection out on the curb, resulting in a heap of rare books exposed to the elements. Eventually by court order, over 800,000 lbs. of books were taken to the city incinerator to be burned.

Image Source: (1) Poughkeepsie Public Library District, date unknown; (2) Poughkeepsie Journal. 1961

Route 9’s construction took place between 1963 and 1966. As a result, 228 tax parcels, including 178 dwellings housing 200 families were destroyed, causing a $650,000 loss to the city’s tax base. The cloverleaf overtook the western half of Union Square. The highway cut through some of the most socioeconomically disadvantaged communities, including a largely Italian population and communities of color.

Image Source: (1) Poughkeepsie Journal. 1965; (2) Poughkeepsie Urban Renewal Agency. 1970

Nearly 20 years after initial plans for a North-South arterial through the City of Poughkeepsie, the expansion of Route 9 was finally completed in 1966. While the highway’s intended purpose may have been to enhance downtown, its effect was instead to hasten suburbanization and impede pedestrian access to the waterfront. Today, the awkwardly conceived left-exit strategy of the double cloverleaf design is the largest source of automobile accidents in all of Dutchess County.
