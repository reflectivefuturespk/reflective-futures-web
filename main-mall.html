Dead Ends at Main Mall

Poughkeepsie’s Main Mall used federal money to quickly change the character of the historic Main Street commercial corridor. The open-air pedestrian mall was meant to compliment the east-west arterial by separating traffic and business in order to support the city’s central business district. Sadly, a combination of rushed planning, growing competition from suburban shopping centers, and a changing perception of Poughkeepsie’s downtown area caused Main Mall to fail.

Dead Ends at Main Mall: 1960 - 2001

The historic center of Poughkeepsie traces back to the intersection of Main Street and the Albany Post Road (present day Main and Market), where markets, businesses and community institutions sprang up and and developed outwards. Since 1811, Main Street has been connected with the Hudson River, and has remained a mixed-use commercial corridor for the City.

Image Source: Library of Congress. 1900

In the 20th century, the increased use of the automobile had an impact on Main Street. The street served as the main east-west vein of traffic and the strong central business core. In the late 1940s, concerns over the heavy traffic and parking became an important issue to Poughkeepsians, as the central part of the city was made unpleasant and parking meters were installed.

Image Source: Pinterest, date unknown

First proposed in 1960, Poughkeepsie’s Main Mall was intended to solidify the downtown as the city’s business core and solve traffic issues. Candeub & Fleissig, the planning firm responsible for the 1960 General Development Plan, developed these renderings, argued that “the character of Main Street is changing in such a way as to make the creation of a mall a logical step”, and that a pleasant, pedestrian-friendly environment is needed to re-establish the integrity of Poughkeepsie’s downtown.

Image Source: Candeub & Fleissig General Development Plan. 1960

By 1968, Poughkeepsie’s Chamber of Commerce raised enough funds to hire Gruen Associations to develop a Downtown Interim Improvement Program. Gruen Associates’ report was ready in 1969, and made the proposal for the mall to be installed as quick as possible. In November 1969, 42 elected and appointed city officials visited Kalamazoo, MI to visit the first pedestrian mall and assess its feasibility in Poughkeepsie. The trip resulted in the unanimous decision that an open-aired mall would work in Poughkeepsie.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Journal. 1969

Plans for Main Mall culminated in 1972 with a pedestrian mall on Main Street between Market and Hamilton. Construction began in 1973 and was finished by 1974. In total, the mall cost $3.2 million. However, most of that was spent on utility work, leaving little left over to beautify the surface. Planners thought that the mall would create a “snowball effect” that would incentivize merchants to help improve the surface of the mall. Unfortunately, merchants were uncreative and viewed the mall as a “freebie” from the federal government, causing the mall to go un-updated.

Image Source: Courtesy of Josh Simons. 1973

Main Mall opened in 1974 just in time for the holiday season. Initially, the mall was a great success, with people flocking from all over the county to come shop. Main Street’s history continued to be featured along the mall, especially with the iconic “Main Mall Row”, a group of 8 commercial buildings on Main Street constructed after the fire of 1870. These historic commercial buildings were listed on the National Register of Historic Places in 1980.

Image Source: National Register of Historic Places. 1980

The mall’s opening was untimely, as suburban shopping centers with free parking and indoor spaces were stiff competitors. This competition led to the closing of the Luckey Platt department store in 1981, an important store to the development of Main Street. Luckey Platt’s closing marked a 130-year history employing local residents and providing access to various goods throughout its 33 departments.

Image Source: Pinterest, date unknown

The 1980s brought further economic failure to the mall, as the image of the inner city shopping center became associated with crime, drugs, and poverty. By 1989, one out of five storefronts were vacant, with only three of the original stores remaining from its opening. This footage follows the band “Faith No More” as they walk through a deserted Main Mall in 1990.

Video Source: MTV 1990

In 1990, a group of Main Mall merchants and property owners organized as the City of Poughkeepsie Partnership urged the city to reopen the eastern block of the mall, between Academy/Catharine and Hamilton to car traffic. Their goal was to increase business by making the stores more accessible. The eastern block of the mall was removed in 1991, with unanimous support of the city council and planning commission, while the merchants agreed to pay 40% of the removal costs. In 2001, the rest of the mall was completely removed as a part of a $2.5 million city project to revitalize downtown. Main Mall’s 27-year lifetime reflects the quick and poorly-planned decisions made on behalf of the city during the urban renewal era, yet the community-led initiative to reclaim Main Street as a thoroughfare demonstrates an important moment of collective action to change the city’s shape to serve the public need.
