Battle Over The East-West Arterial

The East-West Arterial Highway cut high-speed, vehicular channels through sleepy, residential neighborhoods. In an apparent effort to design the “least disruptive” scheme to move commuters through the city to the Mid-Hudson Bridge, the arterial loop severed safe pedestrian access to downtown, while damning 6 miles of historic, residential real estate to slow deterioration by neglect.

Battle Over the East-West Arterial: 1947-1979

The first plans for an east-west arterial were developed in 1947 when the New York State Department of Public Works proposed to create a recessed highway, intersecting the proposed north-south arterial with a large cloverleaf at the approach to the Mid-Hudson Bridge. The city’s budgeting concerns stalled the construction of both highways, however, and the east-west component was put on hiatus after traffic was partially alleviated by the expansion of the Taconic State Parkway in in 1952.

Image Source: New York State Department of Public Works. 1947

In 1960 City of Poughkeepsie approved a General Development Plan which redesigned the arterials into a loop system. The plan was intended to inform city planning decisions over the next two decades and placed high priority upon new construction to account for an expanding suburban population. With one-way arterials proposed to overlay Mill and Church Streets, the loop system was meant to alleviate traffic on congested streets while anticipating increased traffic flows from numerous directions.

Image Source: Candeub & Fleissig General Development Plan. 1960

In 1964, Governor Rockefeller passed legislation that relieved cities in New York State from having to pay the cost to attain rights-of-way for highway projects. With these budgeting concerns offset to the state, Poughkeepsie could again move forward with plans for an east-west arterial while the north-south arterial at Route 9 was already underway. By 1966, planners at the State Department of Transportation worked with the city to specify plans for the loop system, which proposed to widen Mill and Church Streets, and carve new paths through residential neighborhoods to meet Routes 44/55 in the Town of Poughkeepsie. The plan called for the creation of 30 cul-de-sacs, ending easy vehicular and pedestrian access to the Central Business District along Main Street.

Image Source: Adriance Memorial Library. 1966

In an effort to get residents excited about the East-West Arterial, the city released a brochure that included a list of anticipated objections with preemptive rebuttals. The arterials were painted as the least disruptive option for solving traffic congestion, and the city’s strategy for garnering public approval focused on developing compelling propaganda rather than listening to the concerns of community members whose neighborhoods would be sacrificed to serve the needs of commercial and commuter automobiles.

Image Source: Adriance Memorial Library. 1966

Between 1966 and 1970, residents of Poughkeepsie organized as the Citizens Committee Against the East-West Arterial Highway (or the “Citizen’s Highway Committee”) to express opposition to the arterial loop and called for a public referendum. The group took out ads in the Poughkeepsie Journal that criticized specific Aldermen and Mayor Mitchell for not supporting a public vote, depicting them as being in cahoots with “Greedy Main Street Merchants” to increase taxes and destroy neighborhoods without regard for the city’s residents themselves.

Image Source: (1) Adriance Memorial Library. 1966; (2) Poughkeepsie Journal. 1966

Despite organized citizen opposition, Common Council approved plans for the East-West Arterial on May 5, 1969. Upon the passing, Mayor Richard W. Mitchell said, “Gentlemen, I congratulate you. This has been a difficult issue for the City of Poughkeepsie...I am glad that you have now brought it to a conclusion. I think there have been many people in our City who have been wondering, ‘what are you going to do?’ It is finalized. It is behind us. I hope that we can once again join together and work for the future of this City.” Shortly after the vote, the Citizens Highway Committee sued the city, citing violation of state law due to the lack of specifications in the plan, yet the lawsuit did not go far in court

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Journal. 1969

By 1970, a new Mayor supported the push for a public vote on the arterial highway, and a proposition for the referendum made it onto the November ballot. Yet a former Alderman and former chairman of the Poughkeepsie Park Authority brought the proposition to court, which ruled in their favor. State plans for the highway as approved by the prior Common Council were already underway.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Journal. 1970

From 1970 to 1971, the State began acquiring properties along the planned route. This view east on Main Street drastically changed over the next decade, as most buildings in this photo were cleared for urban renewal efforts, including the Civic Center and the arterial. Controversy ensued as demolition began prior to secured construction funding. The defeat of transportation bond legislation in 1971 put the arterial on hold once again, as other urban renewal projects dependent on the highway’s construction awaited proper access roads.

Image Source: Opdycke. 1971

Construction began on the arterial in earnest in 1975, ripping through many neighborhoods to connect Routes 44/55 with Route 9 and the Mid-Hudson Bridge. The project cost nearly $30 million and displaced 48 homeowners, 59 tenants, and 26 businesses. Three-lane highways would now move traffic through the city as fast as possible.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Journal. 1976, 1977

The east-west arterial opened in November of 1979, over 30 years after its initial conception, and a decade since its original approval by the Common Council.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Journal. 1979
