Opening to car traffic in 1930, Poughkeepsie’s second endeavor to span the Hudson River was also the second vehicular bridge to do so, following closely on the heels of the Bear Mountain Bridge, 30 miles south.

The Second Bridge: 1922 onward

Since the completion of the Poughkeepsie-Highland Railroad Bridge in 1889, residents of both sides of the river wanted to be able to cross by foot, yet requests were repeatedly denied. With an expanding population and the growing use of cars, it became clear by the 1920s that another bridge would be required to transport pedestrians and vehicles across the river.

Image Source: AmericanRails.com 1900

In 1922, the economic boom pushed forth the movement to span the Hudson with a vehicular bridge. The ferry system was beginning to prove inadequate for cross-river traffic, and Poughkeepsie’s location halfway between Manhattan and Albany made it a prime location.

Image Source: History of the Hudson Valley Bridge Project. 1922

The bridge was constructed by the New York State Department of Public Works, with an elegant gothic design by Modjeski and Moran. Ralph Modjesk had experience in the region, having worked on the Railroad Bridge nearly 30 years prior.

Image Source: (1) Poughkeepsie Public Library District. 1929; and (2) Charles Ebbets. 1929

On August 25, 1930, Eleanor Roosevelt cut the ribbon at the opening ceremony for the Mid-Hudson Bridge, with her husband, then-Governor of New York Franklin Delano Roosevelt also in attendance. The celebration included an auto procession, clambake, and fireworks, welcoming 12,000 cars and 30,000 pedestrians on the first day.

Image Source: Historic Bridges of the Hudson Valley. 1930

From its opening, the Mid-Hudson Bridge charged tolls. For a decade after the bridge opened, the Poughkeepsie-Highland Ferry stayed economically afloat by undercutting the bridge fee, but when the toll dropped in 1941, the ferry would permanently discontinue crossings.

Image Source: Historic Bridges of the Hudson Valley, date unknown

The 3,000-foot-long, parallel wire cable suspension bridge connects Ulster and Dutchess Counties with a 30-foot width and two walkways on either side. In 2015, the Bridge Authority celebrated 85 years and more than 600 million crossings. With only three lanes, rush hour traffic backups may forever be one of the bridge’s primary character-defining features.
