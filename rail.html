Rail

Rail transit delivered wealth and notoriety to the City of Poughkeepsie by granting full participation in the height of 19th century industrialization. With access to regional networks of exchange and a landmark at the Poughkeepsie-Highland Railroad Bridge, the railroad promised a vibrant, metropolitan future.

Rail: 1849-2018

During the 1840s, railroad connections to New York City became critical for industry and commerce to prosper. The Harlem Railroad (pictured in Green) reached Eastern Dutchess County in 1848, scaring Poughkeepsie’s industrialists to press for the completion of the Hudson River Railroad (pictured in Red). By December 31, 1849, the Hudson River Railroad was formally opened for regular traffic between New York and Poughkeepsie.

Image Source: New York Public Library. 1900

The location of the tracks was a matter of great debate, but it was ultimately decided to offset the line inland from the river’s edge in order to achieve a more direct, efficient and shorter path north. The rail-building industry attracted large populations of Irish immigrants to Poughkeepsie, seeking work and a new life during the era of the Great Famine.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Public Library District. 1905

Soon after the north-south rail lines were laid, the issue of crossing the river became the next infrastructural hurdle. Starting in the 1870s, railcars could board a sidewheel ferryboat at in Newburgh to connect to lines at Dutchess Junction and Fishkill, but the transaction was onerous.

Image Source: trainweb.org, date unknown

Through the efforts of influential Poughkeepsie Mayor Harvey Eastman, the Poughkeepsie Bridge Company was chartered to construct a fixed span of the river in 1871. In addition to being a strategic political maneuver, the city provided an ideal site for the bridge because of the relatively even height of the bluffs on either side of the river.

Image Source: Railroad Bridge Bond. 1887

The first footings were planted in 1873, but a number of political, structural and economic obstacles hindered the bridge’s construction. Boat captains objected to the installation of four major pillars into the city’s active, industrial waterway. The river’s incredible depth, width and tidal nature created unprecedented engineering challenges. The economic Panic of 1873 made investment precarious, and the project was halted. Finally in 1886, construction resumed, and the bridge was completed in another two years at a cost of $10,000,000.

Image Source: New York Times. 1887

The bridge was an instant landmark of engineering, containing the longest simple span and the longest cantilever span in the United States at the time of its completion. The first train crossed on December 29, 1888, and the bridge opened to passenger service on January 1, 1889.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Public LIbrary District. 1888

Beyond its practical function to convey freight and passengers, the Poughkeepsie-Highland Railroad Bridge created a defining visual landmark for the city. Poughkeepsie would now be readily associated with an image of progress and strength.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Public Library District. 1905

Boats, 1938; from "Trip down the Hudson River from Albany to New York City"

Video Source: Public Domain video. 1938

Nationally, rail traffic declined throughout the 1950s and 1960s as manufacturing and raw material extraction industries shrank. Passenger air travel expanded immensely in this same era. On May 8, 1974, the bridge caught fire from a spark flying off the breaks of a passing train, causing irreparable damage after years of deferred maintenance.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Journal. 1974

Despite initial efforts to restore rail service, the bridge fell into the uninterested ownership of Conrail soon after the fire, which then proceeded to decommission all lines that had approached the bridge. To get the large liability off their hands, Conrail sold the structure in 1984 to a criminally neglectful individual who went on to use the bridge merely to collect rent from power lines installed by Central Hudson Gas & Electric. In 1998, the bridge was deeded to the Walkway Over the Hudson for restoration as a pedestrian and cyclist bridge, which opened to great fanfare in 2009.

Video Source: Crenson. 1991

Today, Poughkeepsie remains well-connected to New York City, occupying the last stop on MTA Metro-North’s Hudson Line. Poughkeepsie Station, constructed in 1918, also serves Amtrak passengers traveling north to Albany, and was listed on the National Register of Historic Places in 1976.
