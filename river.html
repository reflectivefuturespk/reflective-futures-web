River

The Hudson River is Poughkeepsie’s oldest piece of natural transit infrastructure. While the Dutch would develop their village high on the plateau rather than at the river’s edge, the confluence of the Fall Kill Creek at the Hudson would forever define the city’s growth by its proximity to flowing water.

River

For an immeasurable chapter of history before Henry Hudson sailed its waters, the river that defines this region was called “Mahicantuck” by its native users, or the "river that flows two ways." The Hudson River is a tidal estuary, where sea water from New York Harbor and fresh water runoff from the mountains share confluence for over 153 miles between New York City and Troy. The local population of Algonquian-speaking Wappinger peoples navigated these waters with rafts and dugout canoes.

Image Source: October on the Hudson, Thomas Worthington Whittredge. 1860

The village of Poughkeepsie was built at the crossing of the Albany Post Road and the Fall Kill Creek, initially agnostic to the river below. Rowboats and canoes continued to be the primary means to cross the river until ferry services began at the end of the 18th century. The first docks were built at Upper Landing, Union Store and Davis’s Road from the 1760s-70s, with a flatboat ferry running trips between Poughkeepsie and New Paltz Landing.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Public Library District, date unknown

In 1793, Noah Elting established a ferryboat cable crossing, and in 1819, the state authorized the Poughkeepsie and New Paltz Ferry Company to run horse-powered ferry crossings.

Image Source: Upper Landing Park

In the early 1800s, river freight and passenger travel became increasingly important to commercial activity of Poughkeepsie. By 1811, Main Street was extended down to the river, connecting the primary commercial artery of the city to a public dock, and by the 1830s, the waterfront transformed into a true industrial hub. Matthew Vassar moved his brewery closer to the waterfront because the location made it easier to load ale onto oceangoing vessels.

Image Source: Walkway Over the Hudson 1840

Matthew Vassar and fellow industrialists known as the “Improvement Party” made new economic ventures at the city’s active waterfront, including a short-lived whaling business located at Upper Landing in the 1830s and 40s. This image depicts the Poughkeepsie Marine Railways & Ship Building Yard and the construction of iconic Hudson River Sloops, as barges, steamships and tugboats pass by in the background.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Public Library District, date unknown

After 1851, river traffic reduced considerably due to the completion of the Hudson River Railroad, running north-south to the east of the river. Boat passengers switched to rail, while freight slowly transitioned into rail throughout the rest of the 19th century.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Public Library District, date unknown

The city’s industrial development would be redirected to the rail line, yet the waterfront remained heavily industrial. Iron furnaces and heavy machinery powered by coal transformed parts of the riverfront into a grimy, noisy, and smoky place, where recreational activities like swimming were abandoned.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Public Library District. 1889

With the completion of the Poughkeepsie-Highland Railroad Bridge in 1889, east-west freight transport could largely bypass boat travel, yet while passenger trains traveled these rails as well, the bridge provided no pedestrian crossing. The Brinckerhoff Ferry, pictured here would continue to run regular trips between the Main Street Dock and Highland Landing until the vehicular tolls on the Mid-Hudson Bridge dropped below 50 cents in 1941.

Image Source: Detroit Publishing Company. 1908

Today, the river continues to be used for oversized freight transport, as well as for recreation. The rowing community in particular carries on a sporting tradition in Poughkeepsie that dates back to the 1830s.
