Early Roads

The name Poughkeepsie developed from a Wappinger term meaning ‘lodge-covered, little-water place’ - referencing a trail and creek-crossing. Dutch settlers called the area rust plaets, or resting place, because it was a landmark for hunters and travellers, rather than a permanent settlement. When the Albany Post Road was constructed after 1703, it followed these native trails.

Early Roads

In 1683, an Indian Deed of Sale relinquished 85,000 acres of what would become Dutchess County, NY from the native Wappinger peoples to three Dutch settlers named Rombout, Van Courtlandt, and Verplanck. Poughkeepsie would only grow into a village after 1703, when the Albany Post Road was developed through what is now downtown, connecting Manhattan and Albany. The road allowed for marketplaces to spring up, bringing settlers, farmers and traders into a central location for commercial exchange. The city’s early settlement had no critical orientation to the river until Union Street connected the intersection of Market and Main Streets to a river landing in the 1770s.

Image Source: Colles. 1789

The Dutchess Turnpike, completed in 1805 and Beekman’s Pawling Turnpike, completed in 1811, allowed access for more trade routes over land by horse and wagon. Main Street was extended down to the waterfront by this time, with a definitive town center growing at the intersection of Market Street, stretching to the south.

Image Source: Library of Congress Geography and Map Division. 1834

Visible here is the short stretch of Main Street, looking east, between the intersections of Market and Washington Streets. In the 1830s, Poughkeepsie’s streets and sidewalks were paved with brick and stone by a group of local industrialists who called themselves the “Improvement Party.” Headed by Matthew Vassar, of Vassar Brewing and later Vassar College, the Improvement Party aimed to promote new industry, develop workable infrastructure, and establish private academies. These improvements spurred growth in Poughkeepsie, and the village was incorporated as a City in 1854.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Public Library District. 1852

The economic boom of the 1920s brought changes to Poughkeepsie’s oldest roads. Pictured here is the Intersection of Main and Market Streets in the 1920s. An early solution for North-South traffic was to modify the streetwall at the Pomfret House to make it easier for cars to travel through downtown. Eventually, New Market Street (today’s Civic Center Plaza) was created to make this thruway more prominent in the city’s street grid.

Image Source: Ghee and Spence, date unknown

Changes to the streetscapes in Poughkeepsie increased interest in city planning, and Chicago planner Myron West was invited to create a comprehensive plan for Poughkeepsie in 1924. West noted that the city’s main thoroughfares were laid out on winding trails, and that plotted subdivisions had little relation to each other, which led him to say “the city is consequently full of street misfits, difficult to remedy or extend in a logical manner.” As a result of the plan, city businessmen sought to widen and extend streets to make them more favorable for automobile traffic. In addition, sights were set on completing a vehicular bridge spanning the Hudson, visible on the left of this 1956 aerial.
