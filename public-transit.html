Public Transit

Technologies of rapid transit are often what distinguish the city from the town. For the City of Poughkeepsie, changing modes of public transportation reflect broader technological and economic shifts, while also tracing profound transformations in local urban capacities to invest in shared space.

Public Transit: 1870's onward

By the 1870s, Poughkeepsie’s population had nearly doubled, swelling from 11,000 to 20,000 since its incorporation in 1854. Its rising class of industrialists and working families made use of horse-drawn trolleys to travel the length of Main Street to visit shops and get to the river for the ferry. By 1872, this service was extended beyond the city limits to the recently established Vassar College for women.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Public Library District. 1894

In 1894, J. W. Hinkey purchased the City Railroad and electrified its cars, extending service loops into neighborhoods to the north and south. Shoppers and workers alike used these lines to travel to the burgeoning downtown area at Market and Main. Most promising was the trolley’s extension 10 miles south to the village of Wappingers Falls, which supported a significant population of textile and garment workers at factories there.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Public Library District. 1894

The trolley allowed for connections to the ferries on the Hudson River as well as to other rail lines to the west and the south. Riders could enjoy short day trips to Poughkeepsie Rural Cemetery or other local destinations like New Paltz and Lake Mohonk by way of the Poughkeepsie-Highland Railroad Bridge. At the height of operations, twenty-five trolley cars seated thirty to fifty people each. Serving commuters and pleasure-riders alike, a diverse mixture of social classes rode the trolley together.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Public Library District. 1930

Video Source: Crenson. 1991

The line to Wappingers Falls closed down in 1928 due to declining ridership after the Albany Post Road was widened, becoming Route 9. The trolley made its final trip down Main Street in 1935. The Poughkeepsie and Wappingers Falls Railway Co., still run by the Hinkley family, transitioned to provide bus service for the city until 1954.

Image Source: Courtesy of Paul Hesse, date unknown

Throughout the 1950s and 1960s two bus companies served the city, with Empire Bus Lines Inc. operating along Main Street and the Poughkeepsie Transit Lines Inc. operating routes on the north side of the city and south to Vassar College in the Town of Poughkeepsie.

Footage Source: Vassar College. 2011

By the end of the 1960s, however, citizen complaints about the quality of vehicles and reliability of services provided by both Empire Bus Lines and Poughkeepsie Transit Lines caused the city to file complaints against both companies. With budgeting concerns, the city was not yet interested in establishing a city-run system at this time, yet by the end of 1971 the New York State DOT had revoked both contracts held by Empire Bus Lines and Poughkeepsie Transit lines, leaving the city without bus service.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Journal. 1969

In 1972, 21-year old Gary Sardone opened Wayfarer Bus Lines, returning public transportation operations to the city after nearly a year. By 1973, the city agreed to purchase a new fleet of buses, to be operated by Wayfarer Lines. Yet continuing complaints eventually led to a 1974 approval for a single city-run bus system, which ultimately operated until 2017.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Journal. 1973

In 2017, Mayor Rob Rolison vetoed the city council’s resolution to continue funding the city bus system. On the horizon was the opportunity to consolidate the city and county bus systems, and the Mayor cited the city’s deficit of $12.5 million as his driving rationale for the merger. Nevertheless, considerable opposition was expressed to the proposal due to its radical reduction of routes--and particularly those that served lower-income communities on the north side of the city. Despite great public controversy, the decision to merge with Dutchess County’s bus system moved forward, and the combined system went into effect by the end of 2017. Since then, additional routes and new vehicles have been added with the aim to provide improved services to communities in greater need.

Image Source: Poughkeepsie Journal. 2017
